CodeBytes: Social Network for Codersmore_vert

CodeBytesclose

CodeBytes is a community for coders, especially those with less-than-extensive backgrounds in the field. It is a place where programmers can post questions or share epiphanies along with Github gists and get feedback from fellow coders.

After two and a half weeks of intense toiling and (too) many late nights, my team and I created CodeBytes, an app built with AngularJS and Foundation on the front-end, along with Satellizer, angular-gist, angular-ui-router, moment, ngTagsInput, and of course, jQuery. Our back-end was built on Ruby on Rails.

Some very cool and challenging features I got to implement with my partner on the front-end were nested comments built out from scratch (with help from here and here) and auto-completed tags when the user submits a post. Our back-end team pulled the top 100 tags from Stack Overflow's API to seed built-in tags and we used ngTagsInput to get the feature fleshed out. It was incredible to see how much we could accomplish in a little over a couple weeks and I enjoyed immensely exploring and researching concepts that I hadn't yet worked with on past projects.

Reeadr: Book-Tracking Appmore_vert

Reeadrclose

Reeadr was completely inspired by the Goodreads app. For a bookworm like me, finding Goodreads was like stumbling on the pot of gold and I wanted to try making an app with similar functionality at a basic level.

This was probably my first independent AngularJS project and when I was given free reign to create an app of my choosing, I vacillated between a tracking apps for either board games or books, two things which I love to do in my free time. I went with books because ISBNs were so easy to get data from. Once I found the Open Library Covers API, I knew that going with books was a no-brainer. I hope to contribute in the future to the BoardGameGeeks app.

The Ginger Monkclose

The Ginger Monk is a website for a fictitious upscale pub. Going with the Irish theme, our group came up with a fun name and logo.

This app was our maiden voyage into the seas of building in teams. Prior to this, we had only worked on functions with one partner, to learn how to avoid merge conflicts. Oh, those dreaded conflicts. Well, I was extremely pleased to experience very little of this, as the project was divided in such a manner as to ensure all group members touched different aspects. I had fun working with the Google Maps and Flickr APIs, as well as filtering a mock API created by our instructor.

France: December 2014close

This app was used to showcase the trip I made to France in December 2014. I built it using Backbone, Handlebars, and Materialize.

If you haven't figured it out, I am not the greatest of designers. One thing I have been worried about taking the path of a front-end developer is that I don't truly have an eye for design. The best I can usually do is admit when something is less than attractive, but beyond that, I have difficulty in beautifying design. This is where designers and CSS frameworks come to save the day! Truly, there is no replacing actual UI/UX designers, who have an immensely important job. But when I am left to fend for myself, frameworks make my projects look a bit more aesthetically pleasing. I had fun reliving my two weeks in Paris and the Brittany region.
